Quant Macro Strategies: EM FX Factor Strategy

A.   Rationale and stylized facts

 

a.    Rationale

 

EM is often invested as a single asset class, offering yield and diversification.
However, it comprises very diverse countries when looking at the structure of their economies.
This variability in macroeconomic fundamentals should drive their assets’ relative performance.

Exhibit 1. Plotting real GDP growth vs. headline inflation, past 5-year average levels in EM and G10 illustrates the far greater variability in macroeconomic data among EM countries than among G10 universe.
Therefore, alpha could be generated by exploiting cross-sectional differences via long-short portfolios – and this is especially true and easy to implement in FX markets.

Source: Haver Analytics

Exhibit 2. Running Principal Component Analysis on EM FX returns in directional terms, we find that a single factor explains 41% of the variance (USD risk), the explanatory power of the following factors increments by < 8%.
This reflects the risk-on/risk-off dynamics of EM flows, and their sensitivity to communal global factors such as core G3 rates, dollar value, international capital flows, etc.
Yet, when looking at EM FX returns in relative terms (i.e. FX return of 1 country vs. average return of all EM FX), the first factor only explains 18% of the variance, and the remaining factors are more balanced.
Relative value in EM FX is worth exploring, since it may be more driven by idiosyncratic, country specific risks (including their macro economy) than by global risk factors.

b.    Stylized facts

 

Stylized facts about EM FX: buy and hold strategy (monthly rolling long basket of 1M FX Forwards/NDFs) in a basket of 21 of the most liquid EM FX yielded a long-term Sharpe close to 0.60 with 6% realized volatility, however drawdown episodes are significant (max peak-to-trough -24.7%) and can last for several years (e.g. 2013-2016).
The long-only return compares well with MSCI EM FX Index, a total return index which comprises around 20 economies as well, but with weights skewed to Asian economies (same weights as in MSCI EM Equity Index).

b.    Potential candidates

 

                                                             i.      Carry and monetary policy related

 

Direct measures – FX market implied carry i.e. log(Forward/Spot), either nominal or real (i.e. adjusted by inflation estimates), interest rates differentials (nominal, real), risk-adjusted carry (e.g. ratio of carry to realized volatility or to implied volatility), etc.
Rationale: FX forwards are biased predictors of future spot levels, therefore, investors can earn the implied carry.

We can also correct the interest rates differentials by the inflation rate as very high inflation can be conducive to currency depreciation in the medium-term, therefore hampering earned carry.

Indirect measures – Carry is impacted by excess inflation (current/estimate inflation vs. monetary policy target), excess stimulus (e.g. excessive monetary aggregates growth, excessive credit expansion, etc.) stemming higher inflation.
Rationale: excessive growth of money supply or excessive credit creation by banks may lead to higher inflation therefore deteriorating the carry earned from holding FX forwards

We can use M1 growth, in real terms (adjusted by headline inflation), and compared to real GDP growth. Alternatively, we can focus on credit creation by using measures similar to BIS’ credit to private non-financial sector, with similar adjustments.

                                                          ii.      Valuation and fundamentals related

 

External balance – current account balance, trade balance, external financing requirements (C/A + net FDI flows), etc.

Rationale: FX is the equilibrium price that clears international markets for goods, services and assets.
A trade deficit, or more widely a current-account deficit, means a country needs to borrow from abroad against its own currency, therefore depreciating the value of its currency. This is fine in case the country imports capital goods that will spur its economy in the future, or if the borrowing requirements are met by capital inflows (such as portfolio flows, FDIs, etc.).
But large (compared to GDP), and persistent current-account deficits should be seen as negative for a currency value.
Change in trade balance can be a form of higher frequency (monthly) assessment of C/A trends (quarterly).

International purchasing power of the currency – REER, PPP, terms-of-trade, etc.

Rationale: high REER means high purchasing power vs. peers but also higher costs for local production, it hurts national competitiveness, it leads to lower net exports, deteriorating the trade balance and current-account.
Against this, the country can take action to weaken its currency, or via monetary channel by lowering interest rates, therefore leading to lower REER. Because of this dynamics, REER should be mean reverting, i.e. any REER misalignment vs. its long-term average should correct.

We can implement this by looking at the current level of REER vs. a long-term average to measure the undervaluation or overvaluation of the currency.

Economic competitiveness – real GDP growth, early growth indicators, industrial production, PMIs, etc.

Rationale: better economic performance means that (1) the monetary policy will be more stable as it does not have to be loosened to spur growth (stable value), and (2) the economy has good productivity and therefore competitiveness which should strengthen its next exports and push its currency to appreciate.

We can use latest print of real GDP growth, of past few quarters’ average, we can also look at the differential of GDP growth in the risk country vs. the base country, vs. the average of EM world, and vs. its potential growth (available for OECD countries).

c.     Initial selection of underlying factors

 

We run OLS regressions between forward-lagged returns (i.e. next 1, 2, 3, 6 months) and the factors we constructed.
We run a regressor utility test (or a positive correlation test) and verify whether the beta is significant (high t-stat, low p-value).
We pick factors sufficiently diversified to reflect the potential drivers of returns. The weighting between the factors will then be done endogenously, within the strategy.
